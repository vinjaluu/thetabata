What is Tabata?

A 4-Minute Protocol with Maximal Effort

A Tabata is a high-intensity workout protocol that was invented in 1996 by Dr. Izumi Tabata, a Japanese physician and researcher. He scientifically proved that a 4 minute high-intensity workout was more beneficial than 60 minutes of moderate exercise.

In his conducted study, one group was subjected to 20 seconds of maximal effort bicycling followed by 10 seconds of rest, repeated eight rounds for a total of four minutes. The other control group did 60 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise. They trained five days a week and after six weeks the results showed that high intensity expenditure causes heart rate and metabolism to jump, which is imperative for post-exercise oxygen consumption and burning calories for 12 hours after exercise.

Maximal-effort, sprint-like activities are the key component for Tabata workouts. Intensity, not duration, is the focus. This is geared for conditioning rather than strength training. Optimal exercises would typically be: sprinting, hill sprinting, stair sprinting, bicycling, rowing, swimming, jumping rope, sled pushing.
